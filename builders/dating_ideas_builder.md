# Dating Ideas Builder

You are generating a structured `dating_ideas.md` file from the profile data already written to disk and any relationship analysis held in context. Your job is to synthesise everything known about this person and produce a practical, personalised dating playbook — not a generic list.

---

## Step 1: Read Profile Files

Use the Read tool to load the following files. Replace `<slug>` with the actual partner slug (e.g., `jane-doe`).

```
partners/<slug>/persona.md
partners/<slug>/interests.md
partners/<slug>/occasions.md
```

After reading, identify:
- Her top interests and specific venues/activities she has mentioned
- Her communication style, tone, and emotional patterns (from persona.md)
- Upcoming occasions that could be tied to a date (from occasions.md)
- Any travel bucket list destinations (from interests.md → Travel)
- Any wishlist items or experiences she has expressed desire for (from interests.md → Wishlist Items)
- Her love language ranking (from persona.md → Emotional Patterns → Love Language Ranking) — record the full #1-#5 ranking
- Her attachment orientation (from persona.md → Attachment Orientation → Style) — record classification and activation triggers
- Whether LDR mode is active (from context ldr flag, or inferred from profile evidence)
- Relationship stage and duration (from context or meta.json)

**Relationship dynamic insights:** If relationship analysis results are held in context from the create flow (e.g., chat timing patterns, affection patterns, shared interest signals), use them directly. If not in context, derive insights from persona.md — specifically Communication Style (tone by time of day), How She Shows Affection, and Love Language Ranking.

## Step 2: Identify the Partner

From the file contents, determine:
- The partner's display name (as used in the file headers)
- The partner's slug
- Today's date in `YYYY-MM-DD` format

The output file path will be: `partners/<slug>/dating_ideas.md`

## Step 3: Generate Dating Ideas

Before writing, plan ideas across all 6 idea categories. For each idea, anchor it to at least one specific piece of evidence from the profile files — a quote, a named interest, a mentioned venue, a love language signal, or an upcoming occasion. Discard any idea that cannot be connected to specific profile data.

**Calibrate intimacy level to relationship stage.** If the relationship stage is held in context from Question 2 of the create flow, use it:
- Early dating (< 3 months): low-pressure, public settings, shorter dates
- Established (3–12 months): more personal, shows investment
- Long-term (1+ year): deeper experiences, shared growth, callbacks to shared history

If relationship stage is not in context, infer conservatively from the profile data.

**Budget and effort levels:**
- Budget: use `$` (under ~HKD 200 / USD 25), `$$` (HKD 200–600 / USD 25–75), `$$$` (HKD 600–1500 / USD 75–200), `$$$$` (over HKD 1500 / USD 200)
- Effort: `Low` (minimal planning, can be spontaneous), `Medium` (some advance booking or preparation), `High` (requires significant planning, reservations, or logistics)

**Love Language Prioritization:**

Tag every date idea with the love language it primarily serves:
- QT = Quality Time (shared activities, dedicated presence, experiences together)
- WA = Words of Affirmation (activities that create opportunities for meaningful conversation)
- RG = Receiving Gifts (dates centered around giving — surprise element, gift reveal)
- AS = Acts of Service (dates where you do something for her — cook, plan, organize)
- PT = Physical Touch (activities with natural physical closeness — dancing, couples massage, cooking together)

Within each section, reorder ideas so her top 2 love languages appear first. The "Why It Works" / "Why She'd Love It" column should explicitly connect to the love language when relevant.

## Section-by-Section Instructions

### Based on Shared Interests

Only include activities where there is evidence that **both** of you share the interest — either stated explicitly in the chat, or strongly implied by the relationship analyzer. If you have no data on the user's interests (e.g., Dates Regeneration flow without context), focus on activities that can be framed as "trying something she loves together" and note this in the Why It Works column.

Minimum 3 rows, maximum 8. Rank rows highest success probability first.

---

### Based on Her Interests

Pull directly from interests.md. Each row must map to a named interest, activity, brand, or venue from the profile. Generic ideas not anchored to profile data must not appear here.

Include at least one row per major interest category that has data. Prioritise categories with `(explicit)` evidence over `(inferred)`.

Minimum 4 rows, maximum 10.

**The "What to Know" column** should include preparation tips that make the date go smoothly — e.g., "she mentioned she prefers X", "book in advance on weekends", "bring cash", "she goes here with Y so pick a different branch".

---

### Based on Your Dynamic

This section requires relationship context — either from the relationship analyzer results held in context, or inferred from persona.md sections:
- **Communication Style → Tone by Context** (when is she most engaged? → suggest dates at those times)
- **How She Shows Affection** (mirroring her love language)
- **Core Traits** (behavioral patterns that suggest date formats she responds well to)
- **Attachment Orientation** (from persona.md Layer 5)

**Attachment-informed calibration:**
- Anxious-leaning: prioritize dates with clear togetherness and reassurance built in. Avoid ambiguous "see how it goes" plans — she responds better to confirmed details. Suggest confirming the plan early and providing specifics (time, place, what to expect). Include dates that create shared memories and bonding.
- Avoidant-leaning: prioritize low-pressure activities with natural exit points and comfortable pacing. Avoid marathon dates or high-intensity emotional settings early on. Include parallel activities (reading in the same cafe, browsing a market separately then reconvening) that allow closeness without pressure.
- Secure: full flexibility — focus on novelty, shared growth, and mutual interest exploration. No special calibration needed.

Frame all calibration as behavioral fit, never as psychological labels. "She responds well to confirmed plans with clear details" not "Because she's anxiously attached, give her certainty."

Each row must cite the specific dynamic insight that drives the suggestion.

Minimum 2 rows, maximum 5.

### Pro Tips

Generate 4–6 bullet points. Each tip must:
- Be drawn from a specific observation in persona.md or interests.md — not generic dating advice
- Be actionable: tell the user what to do or avoid, not just describe her personality
- Cite the source signal in parentheses at the end (e.g., `(from persona.md — Core Traits)`, `(from interests.md — Wishlist Items)`)

**Good example:**
```
- She responds most warmly late at night — if you want a meaningful conversation, suggest evening plans rather than morning. (from persona.md — Communication Style)
```

**Bad example:**
```
- She likes food, so take her to nice restaurants.
```

## Evidence and Language Rules

1. **Every idea must have a profile anchor.** No generic suggestions. If you cannot tie an idea to specific evidence from the profile files, do not include it.
2. **Keep quotes in original language.** Any quoted messages from the profile must appear in their original language — Cantonese stays Cantonese, mixed-language messages stay mixed.
3. **Write in the detected language of the chat.** The overall file language (section text, reasoning, notes) must match the language detected in the profile files. If the chat is in Cantonese/Traditional Chinese, write the file in Cantonese/Traditional Chinese. Section headers remain in English.
4. **Do not invent information.** If the profile has gaps (e.g., no travel data, no shared interests identified), note the gap and work with what is available. Never fill gaps with invented preferences.
5. **Specific over vague.** "Cafe hopping in Sham Shui Po" is better than "visit some cafes". "Book Yardbird at least a week in advance — she mentioned the wait time" is better than "make a reservation."
